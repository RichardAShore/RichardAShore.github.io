Logic seminar, Fall 2012




The topic for this semester will be computable model theory. We will concentrate on the model theoretic rather than the sturcture theoretic one in the sense that we will mostly start our invesitgations with theories and models given effectively with their full elementary diagram (decidable models). We will investigate classical model theoretic theorems and their effective content or the difficulty (say in terms of Turing degree) of extracting their content when it cannot be done effectively. Sample basic notions to be investigated include completeness and compactness; classes of special models such as prime, atomic, homogeneous, universal and saturated; categorictiy (both countable and uncoultable) and related conditions. Issues dealing with recurisve models (i.e. only the atomic diagram is recursive) may be considered as time permits and opportunities arise.

With this view in mind the basic reference is
Harizanov, V. S. [1998], Pure computable model theory, in vol. 1 of Ershov, Yu. L., Goncharov, S. S., Nerode, A. and Remmel, J. B. eds., Marek, V. W. assoc. ed. [1998], Handbook of Recursive Mathematics, Elsevier, New York.

This paper has an extensive bibiography. Other papers in the same volume may also be useful. The chapters are all avaialble for downloading through the University library catalog: Handbook of Recurisive Mathematics

Another survey paper is Effective model theory: the number of models and their compelxity B. Khoussainov and R. A. Shore.

An important book is C.J. Ash and J. F. Knight, Computable Sturctures and the Hyperaithmetic Hierrarchy

Tuesday, 8/28: Recursive Presentations of Trichotomous Strongly Minimal Models
  Uri Andrews, University of Wisconsin, Madison
Abstract: For an uncountably categorical theory T, the spectrum of recursive models of T (SRM(T)) describes the dimensions of models of T with recursive copies. I will show that if T is a base example of a theory satisfying the Zilber trichotomy and has finite signature, then either all models of T are recursive, no models or T are recursive, or only the prime model of T is recursive. (Joint with Alice Medvedev.)


Wednesday, 8/29: Spectra of Theories
  Uri Andrews, University of Wisconsin, Madison
Abstract: For a structure A, the spectrum is the set of Turing degrees of isomorphic copies of A. For a complete elementary first order theory T, the spectrum is the set of Turing degrees of models of T. We will compare these notions, in particular showing that there are theory spectra which are not structure spectra and vice versa. We will also discuss what's known about the relationship between the spectrum of T and the model theoretic properties of T. (Joint with Joseph S. Miller and Julia F. Knight)

There will be a dinner with the speaker Wednesday evening, 6:00 pm at Thai Cusine.

Tuesday, 9/4: Computable Structure Theory and Uncountable Linear Orders
  Asher Kach, University of Chicago

Abstract: Though countable linear orders are fairly well understood in the effective setting, the study of uncountable linear orders in the effective setting is rather recent. After motivating and introducing computability theory within the context of larger domains, we will compare and contrast the computable structure theory of countable and uncountable (mainly size $\aleph_1$) linear orders. In particular, we will discuss computable categoricity, degree spectra, and external relations (such as the successor, infinitely-far-apart, and uncountably-far-apart relations). This work is joint with Noam Greenberg, Steffen Lempp, and Daniel Turetsky.

Wednesday, 9/5: It's All Relative, Except When It's Not: Categoricity versus Relative Categoricity
  Asher Kach, University of Chicago

Abstract: The notions of computable categoricity and relative computable categoricity (also their relativizations) are both rather similar and rather different. In this talk, we attempt to develop both themes, though much of the evidence will point to these notions being rather different. As part of this analysis, we resolve the index set complexity of the computably categorical structures, showing that it is $\Pi^1_1$-complete. This work is joint with some of and all of Rodney Downey, Steffen Lempp, Andrew Lewis, Antonio Montalban, and Daniel Turetsky.

Tuesday, 9/11: Reverse Recursion Theory and d-r.e. Degrees
  Li Wei, National University of Singapore and Cornell University
Abstract: In this talk, we will review some background and history of Reverse Recursion Theory and talk about some results of d-r.e. degrees in that theory. We will show that \Sigma_1 Collection, which is strictly weaker than \Sigma_1 Induction, is sufficient for the existence of proper d-r.e. but interestingly, \Sigma_1 Induction is necessary for the existence of proper d-r.e. degrees below 0'. Moreover, we will show that in some model of \Sigma_1 Collection without \Sigma_1 Induction, every degree below 0' is r.e.

Tuesday, 10/30: Comparing the Complexity of Unstable Theories
  Maryanthe Malliaris, University of Chicago


Abstract: Model theory brings a unique and powerful point of view to bear on many fundamental structural questions in mathematics. This talk will have two interrelated aims. The first is to motivate and present some recent progress of Malliaris on Keisler's order: a suggested program of comparing the complexity of theories from asymptotic (ultrapower) points of view. These results, the first in decades in this program, build towards a true classification of the unstable theories. The stable theories have been well understood thanks to work of Morley and to Shelah's significant generalizations of Morley's work.
The second is to indicate how our work arising from this model theoretic program has led to theorems in Szemeredi regularity and in set theory and general topology. Cantor proved in 1874 that the continuum is uncountable, and Hilbert's first problem asked whether it is the smallest uncountable cardinal. A program developed to measure the continuum in terms of various related cardinals. By Godel 1939 and Cohen 1963, Hilbert's first problem was proved independent of ZFC. However, several basic questions about these cardinal invariants of the continuum remained open despite much investigation. The oldest of these is whether "p = t", proved in a special case by Rothberger 1948, building on Hausdorff 1934. Very recently, Malliaris and Shelah resolved this question, in a surprising way, using model-theoretic tools developed for the study of Keisler's order.

We will take the speaker to dinner after the seminar.

Wednesday, 10/31: Canonization of Borel equivalence relations
  Marcin Sabok, Instytut Matematyczny Uniwersytetu Wroc and Instytut Matematyczny Polskiej Akademii Nauk

Abstract: I will discuss some recent developments in canonical Ramsey theory in the context of descriptive set theory. I will survey canonization results for various classes of equivalence relations. I will show that hypersmooth equivalence relations canonize to three equivalences: two trivial ones and $E_1$. I will also show that equivalence relations reducible to $E_2$ also canonize to three equivalences: the two trivial ones and $E_2$.

Tuesday, 11/6: Recent progress in parameterized upper and lower bounds
  Rod Downey, Victoria University of Wellington

Abstract:
The focus of parameters has delivered tools to the algorithm designers such as kernelization, iterative compression, bounded nondeterminism techniques, color coding, and logical metatheorems. It also has delivered a classification hierarchy of hardness cores based on aspects of logical depth.
Remarkably, in the last decade there has been a matching of upper and lower bound results, more or less up to big-O factors for a number of problems based on complexity assumptions like ETH. There has also been progress showing that certain techniques such as kernelization cannot be used for to give optimal running times for problems, in spite of the fact that FPT is the same as kernelizability.
This talk will look at some of these developments.
There will be a dinner with the speaker after the talk.

Wednesday, 11/7: The computability theory of the Finite Intersection Principle
  Rod Downey, Victoria University of Wellington

Abstract:
A family of sets ${\mathcal F}=\{A_i\mid i\in J\}$ has FIP iff for any finite $I\subset J$, $\cap_{i\in I}A_i\ne \emptyset.$ The FIP principle says that any collection of sets has a maximal subcollection with FIP. The reverse maths and effective content of this principle, classically equivalent to choice, was initiated by Dzharfarov and Mummert. They showed, for instance, that there is a computable family with no computable maximal FIP subfamily. We say that a degree ${\bf a}$ is FIP iff it has the ability to compute a solution to any computable instance of FIP. Dzharfarov and Mummert's result says that ${\bf 0}$ is \emph{not} FIP. Dzharfarov and Mummert showed that each nonzero c.e.\ degree is FIP, all FIP degrees are hyperimmune, and several classes of 1-generics degree were FIP.
We extend these investigations by giving new (shorter) proofs of some of Dzharfarov and Mummert's work, and also showing that all 1-generics are FIP, and showing that, for the $\Delta_2^0$ degrees, that the FIP degrees are precisely the ones that bound 1-generic degrees. We could hope that this characterization extends to the general hyperimmune case, since hyperimmune degrees resemble $\Delta^0_2 $ ones. However, we can also show that there is a minimal $\Delta_3^0$ FIP degree. This last result has some technical complexity as it is a full approximation argument constructing a hyperimmune $\Delta_3^0$ degree, which does not seem to have occurred in the literature hitherto.
(joint work with Diamondstone, Greenberg, and Turetsky)
